Common Questions

Honest answers to the questions people actually ask. If your question isn't here, let us know.

Why should I care about 1948?

Because 1948 is the origin point of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict — and understanding the origin is essential to understanding everything that followed. Every negotiation, every war, every UN resolution traces back to what happened that year. If you care about the Middle East, human rights, or international law, you should care about 1948.

Isn't this just pro-Palestinian propaganda?

No. This site does not tell you what to think. It presents documented evidence — UN resolutions, census data, maps, court rulings, and academic research — and lets you draw your own conclusions. Every claim is footnoted with its source. If you find an error, tell us and we'll correct it. That's the editorial promise.

If the documented facts make you uncomfortable, that's not propaganda — that's cognitive dissonance. We invite you to investigate it rather than dismiss it.

Didn't Palestinians leave voluntarily in 1948?

Some did; many did not. The historical record — including Israeli state archives declassified in the 1980s — shows that a significant number of Palestinians were forcibly expelled by Zionist militias and later the IDF. Others fled in fear after massacres like Deir Yassin. Some left temporarily expecting to return — and were never allowed back.

Israeli historian Benny Morris, who is hardly pro-Palestinian, documented dozens of specific expulsions in The Birth of the Palestinian Refugee Problem (1988), concluding that "the displacement was not an unintended byproduct of war but a result of specific military operations."

What about the Jews expelled from Arab countries?

Between 1948 and the 1970s, approximately 850,000 Jews left or were expelled from Arab countries. This was a genuine tragedy, and their stories deserve to be told. However, one injustice does not justify another. The displacement of Palestinians in 1948 and the displacement of Arab Jews are separate historical events with different causes and contexts. Acknowledging the Nakba does not erase the suffering of Arab Jews — it simply tells the full story. Both can be true.

Is criticizing Israel antisemitic?

No. Criticizing the policies of the Israeli government is no more antisemitic than criticizing the policies of the Chinese government is anti-Asian, or criticizing the policies of Saudi Arabia is anti-Arab. Israel is a state, not a religion or an ethnicity. Many Jewish people — including Israeli historians, Holocaust survivors, and organizations like Jewish Voice for Peace — criticize Israeli policies. Antisemitism is real and serious. Using the accusation to shut down legitimate criticism of a state's actions devalues the term and makes it harder to fight actual antisemitism.

Wasn't the land "a land without a people for a people without a land"?

This phrase, widely attributed to early Zionist writers, is historically false. By any measure, Palestine was inhabited — by approximately 500,000 people in the late 19th century, rising to over 1.9 million by 1947. Two-thirds were Arab. They had villages, cities, farms, olive groves, mosques, churches, schools, and newspapers. The idea that Palestine was empty is a colonial myth that served to delegitimize the people who lived there. Even early Zionist leaders who used the phrase acknowledged the Arab presence, often describing it as an obstacle to be overcome.

Why won't Arab countries just absorb the Palestinian refugees?

Several reasons. First, Palestinians are not from Lebanon, Syria, or Jordan — they are from Palestine. Saying they should be absorbed elsewhere is like telling Ukrainian refugees they should permanently resettle in Poland instead of returning home. Second, many host countries lack the resources to fully integrate millions of refugees. Third, and most importantly, the refugees have a right — affirmed by UN Resolution 194 and reaffirmed more than 130 times — to return to their homes or receive compensation. This right is not extinguished by the passage of time or by an assumption that "they should just move on."

What about the Holocaust? Doesn't that justify Israel?

The Holocaust was one of the greatest crimes in human history. Six million Jews were murdered. The need for a safe haven for Jewish people after centuries of persecution — culminating in the Holocaust — is real and profound. But the question is not whether Jewish people deserved safety and self-determination. The question is whether that safety and self-determination required the displacement of another people from their homes. These are two separate moral claims. Acknowledging the horror of the Holocaust does not require ignoring the Nakba. Both matter. Both deserve to be understood.

We don't argue. We document.

What Happened in '48 is a non-advocacy documentation project. We collect, verify, and present evidence — and let it speak for itself.
